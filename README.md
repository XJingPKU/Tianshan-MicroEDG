# Tianshan-MicroEDG (Elevational diversity gradients)

This repository contains data and R script for the manuscript entitled "Deeper is not safer: Soil depth intensifies environmental filtering and exposes hidden microbial vulnerability along an elevational gradient"

**Abstract**  
Aim: A pervasive but rarely tested assumption in soil biogeography is that surface soils faithfully represent the belowground biosphere and that the physical stability of deeper soils buffers subsurface microbial communities against environmental perturbation. We aim to test whether soil depth systematically reshapes microbial biogeographical patterns and their underlying drivers along elevational gradients, establishing a critical baseline for predicting subsurface biodiversity vulnerability under global change.  
Location: Tianshan Mountains, China  
Time period: 2019  
Major taxa studied: Soil bacteria and fungi  
Methods: We sampled soil bacteria and fungi across 0–50 cm soil profiles along a 60-km elevational transect (465–3035 m) in the Tianshan Mountains.  
Results: Both bacterial and fungal diversity exhibited hump-shaped elevational patterns that became more pronounced with increasing soil depth. Mechanistically, and contrary to the prevailing expectation that stable subsurface conditions reduce ecological constraints, environmental filtering explained a progressively greater fraction of microbial diversity variation with increasing depth: its explained variance increased from 60% to 87% for bacteria (0–50 cm) and from 22% to 50% for fungi (0–30 cm). This intensification was driven by a depth-dependent reorganization of environmental filtering regimes, transitioning from thermal energy dominance at the surface to a collective governance by chemical energy availability, plant diversity, and abiotic filters in subsurface layers.  
Main conclusions: Our findings overturn the assumption that subsurface physical stability insulates deeper communities from environmental shifts. Subsurface microbial communities are more environmentally sensitive, not less, than their surface counterparts, meaning that current surface-parameterized frameworks systematically underestimate their vulnerability to global change. To avoid critical blind spots in risk assessment, conservation biogeographers must transition to 3D modeling and mandate multi-depth biodiversity monitoring. Ultimately, protecting aboveground plant diversity is crucial to sustaining the vertical energy flows that stabilize this highly sensitive, carbon-rich subsurface biosphere.
